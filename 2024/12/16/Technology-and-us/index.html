Technology and Us

  2. let them think and write on their own

Human in inventing technology

  • Start with the agricultural revolution in shaping the human society.
    • Before the agricultural revolution there was the hunting and gathering society, which occupied 90% of the human society.
    • discuss about the hunting and gathering society.
      An useful argument

      Before this, most people tended to view prehistory through the lens of linear progress. Since agriculture emerged later and eventually replaced the hunter-gatherer lifestyle in most parts of the world, it was assumed to represent a form of advancement. As a result, hunter-gatherer societies were considered resource-scarce and subsistence-based, while the invention of agriculture was seen as liberating humanity from extreme dependence on nature.

      However, through in-depth studies of existing hunter-gatherer groups, anthropologists have discovered that their subsistence was not particularly difficult, and they enjoyed significant amounts of free time. Notably, the daytime sleep duration per person in these societies far exceeded that of any other type of society. Compared to farmers who toiled endlessly, they appeared remarkably “lazy.” Furthermore, when measuring energy output per unit of time, farmers were not significantly more productive than hunter-gatherers. Robust evidence supports this view, such as Marshall Sahlins’ Stone Age Economics (1972), in which the first chapter explicitly refers to hunter-gatherers as The Original Affluent Society. This characterization highlights that their material needs were easily met and critiques the infinite desires fostered by modern market economies.

      Moreover, most surviving hunter-gatherer groups today have been pushed to marginal, inhospitable environments unsuitable for agriculture (such as the Bushmen of the Kalahari Desert). Prehistoric hunter-gatherers who lived in more favorable environments likely enjoyed even greater leisure. Additionally, ethnographic studies indicate that hunter-gatherers did not exploit resources intensively. Their population levels were far below the maximum capacity that their resource base and subsistence methods could theoretically support. In other words, they were not as constrained by basic necessities as commonly imagined.

      Archaeological records provide further insights. Unlike the mobile lifestyles of hunter-gatherers, farmers settled in villages where water sources were often polluted. Domestication of animals and other factors led to the spread of infectious diseases. The repetitive physical labor of farming resulted in bodies that were no longer as well-proportioned as those of hunter-gatherers. Farmers suffered from cavities due to high-starch diets, deformed joints from constant strain, and malnutrition from insufficient intake of animal protein. These issues are clearly documented in the skeletal remains excavated from agricultural societies.

  • Actually, human is not always inventing things that are better than the existing ones. Another example is the realism and naturalism in the literary theory.
    • Realism: Emerged in the late 18th and early 19th centuries, inheriting the theory of mimesis. It emphasizes the active reflection and representation of reality in literature—this reflection is not mechanical but involves subjective selection and creation. It aims to reveal the essential laws of social life. For example, Stendhal’s The Red and the Black exposes social structure and issues through the story of a common person’s social ascent.
    • Naturalism: Emerged in the late 19th century, emphasizing objective and scientific truth, focusing on the unconditional reproduction and documentation of nature in literature. It is seen as unlikely to be fully realized and is considered a kind of regression. Key figure: Émile Zola.

Progress in controlling human

Maybe it’s not us making progress, but progress demanding us to contribute to it? The subtle difference here is whether we have the control of it. Can we call for a stop?

As an youtube video showed,

“Progress produces it self but then requires itself so it must consume itself… Today we are so dependent on technology that we can not really let this loop stop.”

What is it from the perspective from an average person? Here is an argument written by myself:

Before I die, I live. To maintain the state of being alive, I need commodities. To acquire commodities, I need money. This most likely means I need to work. Thus, in the process of working and pursuing work, I am forced to submit to discipline.

This is a simplified logical chain for a rational individual in the context of contemporary society. Why simplified? Because the functions of work, money, and commodities extend beyond mere subsistence. Together, they define one’s identity, effectively resolving the question of “Who am I?” In other words, “I” am the cleaner, the programmer, the teacher, the father, or the mother. “I” am my bank balance, the gaze of others upon me, the commodities I purchase, the clothes I wear, and the perfected persona I sculpt to market myself. Going further, as we immerse ourselves in this societal game, our goal extends beyond mere “survival” to achieve dignified survival. That is, we seek to avoid pain and pursue pleasure.

For this, we are willing to sacrifice a great deal. Scoring high on an exam? Landing that high-paying job? Of course, I want these! Why wouldn’t I? Even though I don’t fully understand what they mean, I know they will bring me more pleasure, more of… I don’t know, but certainly better things. You ask what I truly desire? Of course, it’s these lovely commodities! What other answer could there be? Throw yourself into the game! Cast aside thoughts of death—this new laptop is exactly what you need, this new book you’ll never read is what you need, this new house is what you need, marriage is what you need, the pleasure of lying on the couch scrolling through short videos for an entire afternoon is what you need. To obtain all this, you need exams, you need work, you need to abide by all the unspoken rules of society. Isn’t that right?

It’s not?

Then what will you do? At the end of the day, do you have another choice? For instance, pursuing something else—what’s that? You say, “I pursue pain”? What are you talking about? Pain being as precious as pleasure? Shut your mouth; I don’t even want to hear it. You must participate in this game. You must convince yourself: this is what I want. Otherwise, your life will collapse in an instant.

You vaguely sense that this is false. This is an illusion, a means to escape the underlying meaninglessness of life. Yet, you have no other way. The logical chain I laid out at the beginning is so robust that no one can escape it. Thus, the moment you start thinking, you begin to suffer. So, is cheap happiness better, or is noble suffering better? Stop and think about it for a moment. This is the question Dostoevsky raises in Notes from Underground.

The problem is, the former is clearly not what you want, but neither is the latter. You find yourself in a dilemma.

However, this problem is actually solvable—by realizing there are not only two paths. Yes, there can be cheap suffering and noble happiness. Moreover, we can oscillate between happiness and suffering, and this is who I am: I experience the ecstasy of life in the midst of agony.

We ought to embrace agony. Embrace the unknown, the dangerous, the fearful, the unspoken. That is the path I should take, for it allows me to escape falsehood.

You might say, what’s the use of thinking about these things? Discipline is so powerful that we are all trapped within it. After making such a statement, won’t you still wake up at 7 a.m. to go to school and come back at 10 p.m.? Does this have any meaning?

Meaning? Is the purpose of life to pursue meaning? It seems we are drowned by this mainstream narrative: everyone is searching for meaning. No one finds it, so they resort to present pleasures and enjoyments. “Oh, live in the moment. The moment is meaning. Be yourself.” And so on. This is absurd: after saying these things, we throw ourselves even more fervently into material pursuits, willingly enslaved by family, school, corporations, and governments.

Therefore, I say no. To me, life is about pursuing wholeness: pleasure does not make me whole; pain makes me whole. More importantly, only the ecstasy of life and the agony of death can make me whole. Life gives death its possibility; death gives life its completeness.

I live for ecstasy and to die in agony.

original text in Chinese

在死之前，我活着。为了维持活着的状态，就需要商品。为了获取商品，我需要货币。这大概率意味着我需要工作。那么，在工作以及追求工作的路途中，我就要被迫服从规训。

这是当今社会语境下理性个体的一条简化的逻辑链。为什么是简化的？因为工作、货币和商品的功能不仅于此。它们联合起来，完成了对于个体对于其身份的定义，也就是标签化地解决了“我是谁”的问题。换言之，“我”就是那个清洁工，那个码农，那个教师，那个父亲或者母亲；“我”就是我的余额，他人投向我的目光，我购买的商品，那身我穿着的衣服，我用来销售自己而雕塑的那个完美的人格。更进一步，当我们投身社会给我们制定的这一场游戏中，我们的目的便远远不仅是“生存”，而更是要“体面地生存”。这就是说，我们躲避痛苦，追求愉悦。

为此，我们可以牺牲不少东西。在考试中获得高分？获得那份高薪工作？这当然是我想要的！我怎么会不想要呢？虽然我不知道那到底意味着什么，但我知道那正是我想要的。我知道它会给我带来更多的愉悦，更多的……我不知道，但总之是更好的东西。你问我自己究竟想要什么？当然是这些可爱的商品！难道还有别的答案吗？投身这场游戏吧！把死亡抛之脑后，这台新笔记本电脑正是你需要的，这本你并不会看的新书正是你需要的，这套新房子正是你需要的，婚姻正是你需要的，在沙发上躺着刷一个下午短视频所带来的愉悦正是你需要的。为了获得这些，你需要考试，你需要工作，你需要遵守社会中的一切不言自明。不是吗？

不是？

那你要怎么做呢？说到底，你有别的做法吗？比方说，追求别的什么东西，——你说什么？“我追求痛苦”？你在说什么？嗯？不，痛苦怎么可能和愉悦同样珍贵？闭上你的嘴，我简直不想听你说话。你不得不参与这场游戏。你不得不说服自己相信：那就是我想要的。不然，你的生活顷刻就崩塌。

你隐隐地意识到，这是虚假的。这是幻觉，对于生命潜在无意义的一种逃避。然而，你没有别的办法。我在开头抛出的逻辑链如此坚固，没有任何人能逃离。于是一旦思考，你便开始痛苦。那么，是廉价的幸福好，还是崇高的痛苦好？停下来思考一下吧。这是陀思妥耶夫斯基在他的《地下室手记》中发出的疑问。

问题就在于，前者必不是你想要的，后者也不尽然。你陷入了一种困境。

然而，这问题其实好解决，那就是认识到这里不只有两条路。没错，这里可以有廉价的痛苦和崇高的幸福。更进一步，我们可以在幸福与痛苦中来回震荡，这便是我：我在剧痛中体会生命的狂喜。

应该去拥抱剧痛。拥抱那未知的，危险的，恐惧的，被避而不谈的。那才是我应该投奔的道路，因为它允许我逃离虚假。

你会说，想这些有什么用呢？规训如此强力，我们都在其中动弹不得。在发表这么一番言论之后，您照样要早上七点起来上学，晚上十点回家，这有什么意义吗？

意义？生命的目的在于追求意义吗？我们好像被这种主流的声音给淹没了：大家都在追寻意义。没有人能找到，于是只好诉诸于当下的愉悦和享乐：噢，活在当下吧。当下就是意义。做你自己。等等。这简直是一种荒诞：我们在这么说之后变本加厉地投身于物质的追求，为此甘心被家庭、学校、公司、政府所奴役。

因此我要说不。在我看来，生命在于追求完整：愉悦并不使我完整；痛苦使我完整。更进一步说，只有生命的狂喜和死亡的剧痛能够使我完整。生命给死亡以可能，死亡给生命以完整。

我活着，正是为了狂喜，并在剧痛中死去。

每一年都有无数人重复地发现异化这一概念, 而且也实际上意识到了是私有制的等级社会, 而非某种”现代或都市生活”, 造就了这一状况. 但是当需要他们将这一点明确地讲出来时, 大多数人退缩了.
