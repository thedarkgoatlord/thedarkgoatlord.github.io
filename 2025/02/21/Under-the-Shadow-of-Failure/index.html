Under the Shadow of Failure

Piecing myself back together from the bitterness.

Yesterday, I competed in the Senior division of the CCC algorithm contest. Three hours in total. In thirty minutes, I solved the first two problems. For the next two and a half hours, nothing. I tried—yes—but the 0/15 on the screen was unyielding. A cold, heavy helplessness crept over me, slow and suffocating. Ten minutes before the end, I knew it was over. I looked at the screen. At the window. At my classmates. Then I stood up and walked out.

Perhaps I should have practiced more. Practiced segment trees. Studied A*. Thought of enumeration earlier. These thoughts crashed over me the moment I left.

This is not unfamiliar. Last semester, I did HiMCM, a mathematical modelling competition. We worked hard, met every requirement, and used a range of methods—time series analysis, differential equations, convolutional neural networks, polynomial regression. Our explanations were thorough, our structure clear, our results solid. My time series model fit real-world data almost perfectly. We had expectations. We got an M award. That was it.

The year before, I took AMC10. I prepared hard. Scored 106. Past years said that was enough for an award. That year, the cutoff rose beyond 110. Honor Roll Distinction? 139.5. A classmate scored 140 on both A and B tests. I wondered if this was still the AMC I knew. Then I heard about the leaks.

Failure is a constant. I do not dwell on it, but I feel it—something shattering, irreparable. And then I gather the pieces, piece myself back together, and move on.

This is where I should say failure is growth, experience, a stepping stone. That it makes you stronger. That would be neat. But it would not help. The weight of failure, the sting of wasted effort, the chasm between expectation and outcome—these things are real. They do not dissolve with words.

So instead, I walk with failure.

The method is simple: routine. No matter how crushing the loss, it does not disrupt my daily rhythm. Walks, piano, early mornings—they remain untouched.

These are my scaffolding. When I falter, they hold me steady. And so, I continue.

This is why I have done what I have done—an IELTS 7.5, orchestral performances, a full scholarship. Not all of it is conventional success. But I know:

  1. I persisted, and I saw results.
  2. My progress does not stop with a result.

That is enough.
