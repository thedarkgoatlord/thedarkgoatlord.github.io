Computer science. Philosophy. French horn？ And writing of course.

So, who am I?

A thinker. A learner. An action taker. More importantly, a writer. Having strong interests in computer science, literary theory, sociology and philosophy, the challenge I’m currently facing is to balance between them.

This blog is created merely for fun. I’ll be publishing my thoughts and some of the writings, so they can act as a window for anyone willing to know me a little bit more. Maybe I’ll write about my musical interest too? Playing French horn is definitely an important part of my life. I started doing it when I was in primary school, grade 3 I think. Can’t imagine 8 years just passed by. I’ve been in the school orchestra for 6 years or so, with the latest 3 being the section leader. I’m still practicing at a regular basis despite all of this academic stress of grade 11.

It is 2024 December the 6th when I am writing this line of text, and the same day next year you’ll probably see me applying for universities.

Why am I interested in so many things?

The First Question

As you may notice, my interests are kind of…mixed. When I first told my friends that I was going to apply for a major called ‘computer science and philosophy’, the one answer I always get was: ‘Fun. So there actually is such a thing?’

To explain it, we have to go back to my grade 9, when I started wondering about the word ‘intelligence’.

So what is intelligence? Maybe it is the ability to make predictions base on observations? Then how about mathematics? We don’t usually do mathematics by observing. Instead, we use reason. That way, the idea of intelligence has to have some connection with the ability to reason. Then, what is this connection? As I proceed, the questions somehow reproduced: they became more and more, which quickly led me to confusion.

Then, I started reading about artificial intelligence, in the hope that the way we build intelligence on our own can give me inspiration on what intelligence is. Soon, I found myself watching online videos about linear algebra, support vector machine, multilayer perceptron networks, and etc.

Unfortunately, my enquiry into artificial intelligence didn’t provide me with satisfactory answers, although some thoughts were indeed provoked. Basically, there were two major parties in machine learning, which were symbolism and connectionism. For me, they represented two distinct answers to the question ‘what is intelligence’. The symbolists believed that, the essence of intelligence is merely symbolic operations within a system in which the rules of the operations can be explicitly defined. The connectionists, also acknowledging intelligence as a symbolic system, rejected the idea that this system can be explicitly defined. Instead, they sought intelligence by attempting to simulate the structure of a human brain.

Yes, we all can tell, the connectionists did a better job in producing models that are ‘intelligent’. But not even themselves could understand the mechanism of their neural networks: these networks were, to be said, ‘blackbox models’, meaning that no one really knew how they worked. The natural question thus arises: can these networks be someday understood, or is it in essence impossible to be understood?

This question was not answered until grade 11, when I read about Wittgenstein. But wait, how did I even know Wittgenstein? Why did I suddenly started reading about philosophy? Well, let’s save the answer for now.

The second question

After finding myself making no progress on what is intelligence, it was natural for me to turn into another question: what would happen if artificial general intelligence(AGI) is realised? Now, this change in direction may be a little bit abrupt. But recall that back then, I had absolutely no idea what philosophy was, and was planning to apply for computer science (because of the progress I had made in exploring machine learning).

Witnessing what Chat-GPT could do, it was simply impossible for me to not think about AGI. What would happen if AGI is realised? Somehow, my thoughts quickly turned into a reflection on technology in the general sense.

Here is the point: we, human, were making technological advancements without thoroughly thinking about it. And yes, we were NOT in control of it. Instead, we were pushed by it, ‘so the productivity is risen and we can all enjoy a better life’, we thought. But is that true? Is is correct to just push that boundary of technology anyways?

The question inspired me to make this game: ‘Echo’.
